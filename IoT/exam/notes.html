Q1 – Carrier frequency for a 1 mm antenna (antenna = λ/8)
What is the carrier frequency if the antenna length is $L = 1\text{ mm}$ and $L = \lambda/8$?
  • Step 1 – Find wavelength:
    $$\lambda = 8L = 8 \times 1\text{ mm} = 8\text{ mm} = 8 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m}$$
  • Step 2 – Find frequency using $f = c/\lambda$ with $c \approx 3\times 10^8\text{ m/s}$:
    $$f = \frac{3\times 10^8}{8\times 10^{-3}} = 3.75\times 10^{10}\text{ Hz}$$
  • Result: $$\boxed{f \approx 37.5\ \text{GHz}}$$

Q2 – Packets per second at 250 kbps?
How many 20-byte packets per second can be sent at 250 kbps with a 10 ms mandatory gap between packets?
  • Packet size in bits: $20\text{ B} = 160\text{ bits}$
  • Transmission time per packet: $$t_{tx} = \frac{160}{250{,}000} = 0.64\text{ ms}$$
  • One cycle (tx + gap): $0.64 + 10 = 10.64\text{ ms}$
  • Constraint for $n$ packets in 1 s (1000 ms):
    $$n \cdot 0.64 + (n-1)\cdot 10 \le 1000$$
    $$10.64n - 10 \le 1000 \;\Rightarrow\; 10.64n \le 1010 \;\Rightarrow\; n \le 94.92$$
  • Result: $$\boxed{94\ \text{packets/s}}$$

Q3 – Why is QAM popular, and what are its challenges?
  • Why popular:
    • QAM uses both amplitude and phase to encode data.
    • It can send more bits per symbol than simpler schemes (e.g., BPSK/QPSK).
    • Achieves high spectral efficiency — more data in the same bandwidth.
  • Challenges:
    • Higher-order QAM (64-QAM, 256-QAM) has constellation points closer together → more sensitive to noise, interference, distortion.
    • Requires better SNR, more linear amplifiers, accurate synchronization (phase/frequency), and more complex receivers.

Q4 – What is packet switching and what are its advantages?
  • Definition: Data is split into small packets; each packet is routed independently through the network.
  • Advantages:
    • Efficient sharing of network resources (many users share the same link)
    • Robust — packets can take different paths if a route fails
    • Good for bursty traffic (internet-style communication)
    • Scalable and flexible
    • No dedicated circuit reservation required

Q5 – Why do we have different medium access strategies? Why is one not enough?
  • Wireless environments and requirements differ a lot — no single MAC fits all scenarios.
  • Different strategies optimize for different goals:
    • Contention-based (CSMA): Simple, flexible, good for light/bursty traffic
    • Scheduled / TDMA: Predictable delay, better for real-time traffic
    • FDMA / CDMA / OFDMA: Better sharing in dense systems
    • Polling / token-based: Controlled access, fairness
  • Trade-offs that force the choice:
    • Latency vs. throughput
    • Energy efficiency vs. performance
    • Simplicity vs. determinism
    • Low traffic vs. high traffic
    • Short-range IoT vs. Wi-Fi vs. cellular

Q6 – Decompose $P_{rx}/P_{tx}$ into static and dynamic parts (logarithmic approach)
  • Starting point: $$\frac{P_{rx}}{P_{tx}} = \frac{A_{tx}A_{rx}G_{tx}G_{rx}}{d^2}$$
  • Take $10\log_{10}(\cdot)$: $$10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P_{rx}}{P_{tx}}\right) = 10\log_{10}(A_{tx}) + 10\log_{10}(A_{rx}) + 10\log_{10}(G_{tx}) + 10\log_{10}(G_{rx}) - 20\log_{10}(d)$$
  • Decomposition: $$L = \underbrace{\bigl(10\log_{10}A_{tx}+10\log_{10}A_{rx}+10\log_{10}G_{tx}+10\log_{10}G_{rx}\bigr)}_{\text{Static (design-time)}} + \underbrace{\bigl(-20\log_{10}d\bigr)}_{\text{Dynamic (runtime)}}$$
  • Static: Antenna sizes and nominal gains — chosen during design.
  • Dynamic: Distance $d$ — changes at runtime.
  • If gains are controlled by beamforming, they can be moved to the dynamic part.

Q7 – Why choose between 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz in IEEE 802.11?
  • 2.4 GHz:
    • Longer range, better wall penetration
    • More interference (Bluetooth, microwaves, many Wi-Fi devices)
    • Fewer non-overlapping channels
  • 5 GHz:
    • Higher throughput potential
    • More channels / less interference
    • Shorter range, worse wall penetration
  • Choice depends on: Need for range/coverage → 2.4 GHz; need for speed/capacity → 5 GHz.

Q8 – How does dynamic frequency hopping in IEEE 802.15.1 avoid interference?
  • IEEE 802.15.1 (Bluetooth Classic) uses Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS).
  • Transmitter and receiver hop together across many channels in a pseudo-random sequence.
  • If some channels are noisy/interfered, communication only suffers briefly on those hops.
  • With Adaptive Frequency Hopping (AFH), bad channels are detected and excluded from the hop sequence.
  • Result: Better robustness in crowded 2.4 GHz environments (e.g., coexistence with Wi-Fi).

Q9 – What is header compression in IEEE 802.15.4 networks and why do we need it?
  • IEEE 802.15.4 frames are very small (low-power IoT links), so protocol headers can be a huge overhead.
  • Header compression reduces upper-layer headers (IPv6/UDP) by:
    • Removing fields that are known or constant
    • Encoding fields in shorter forms
    • Inferring values from context
  • Why needed:
    • 802.15.4 has a small MTU / payload (≈80–100 bytes after frame overhead)
    • Without compression, headers waste most of the packet
    • Compression improves payload efficiency, energy consumption, and throughput
  • Especially critical for 6LoWPAN over 802.15.4 (RFC 6282 / LOWPAN_IPHC).

Q10 – Reflector problem: determine the received power
A lossless reflector is placed between transmitter and receiver. Each hop follows an inverse-square law $P \propto 1/d^2$. What is $P_{received}$?
  • Power at reflector (first hop): $$P_{reflector} = \frac{P_{transmitter}}{d^2}$$
  • Power at receiver (second hop, lossless reflection): $$P_{received} = \frac{P_{reflector}}{d^2} = \frac{P_{transmitter}}{d^2} \cdot \frac{1}{d^2} = \frac{P_{transmitter}}{d^4}$$
  • $$\boxed{P_{received} = \frac{P_{transmitter}}{d^4}}$$
  • Note: $d$ here is the per-hop distance. If $d$ is the total distance, the result differs by a constant factor.
