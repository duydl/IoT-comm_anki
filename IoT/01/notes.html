A wireless sensor node uses an antenna of length $L = 3\text{ cm}$. Assume $L = \lambda/4$ for efficient radiation. What carrier frequency should be used?
  • Find wavelength: $$\lambda = 4L = 4 \cdot 0.03 = 0.12\ \text{m}$$
  • Find frequency using $f = c/\lambda$, $c = 3\times10^8\ \text{m/s}$: $$f = \frac{3\times10^8}{0.12} = 2.5\times10^9\ \text{Hz}$$
  • $$\boxed{f = 2.5\ \text{GHz}}$$

Name and briefly explain the three main wireless propagation effects. Which one can cause a receiver to get a signal even without line-of-sight?
  • Reflection: Signal bounces off a large object (much larger than wavelength); can cause phase shift and constructive/destructive interference.
  • Diffraction: Signal bends around edges/obstacles.
  • Scattering: Signal is dispersed by objects with size comparable to wavelength.
  • The effect that allows reception without LOS: $$\boxed{\text{Diffraction}}$$

Why can QAM achieve better bandwidth efficiency than simple AM?
  • QAM combines two orthogonal carriers (I and Q channels).
  • Two sub-streams can be transmitted in parallel over the same channel bandwidth.
  • This doubles the spectral efficiency compared to simple AM, which uses only one carrier dimension.

What are the three components of total path loss?
  • Distance-dependent path loss – deterministic decay with distance (e.g., $\propto d^2$ in free space).
  • Shadowing – slow variations due to obstacles (buildings, terrain); modeled as log-normal.
  • Fast fading – rapid fluctuations due to multipath interference; changes over short distances.

Why is modulation needed in wireless communication?
  • Modulation shifts information onto a carrier wave at a specific frequency.
  • This allows efficient transmission over radio and enables multiplexing of many users on different carriers.
  • Baseband signals (e.g., audio, data) cannot be radiated directly — they need to be up-converted to a suitable RF frequency (antenna size ∝ wavelength).

What is the basic idea of Amplitude Modulation (AM)?
  • The amplitude of the carrier wave is varied in proportion to the information signal.
  • Frequency and phase of the carrier remain constant.
  • In digital form: ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying) — discrete amplitude levels represent bits.

What is the relation between antenna size and wavelength for efficient transmission?
  • Efficient antennas are sized relative to the wavelength of the carrier. A common rule: $$L \approx \frac{\lambda}{4}$$
  • Higher frequencies → shorter wavelengths → smaller antennas (e.g., 2.4 GHz → ~3 cm; 37.5 GHz → ~1 mm).
  • This is why IoT devices operating at higher frequencies can be very compact.

What is the basic idea of Frequency Modulation (FM)?
  • The frequency of the carrier wave varies according to the information signal.
  • The amplitude remains approximately constant.
  • FM is more robust to amplitude noise than AM, since information is in frequency, not amplitude.

What is FSK?
  • FSK (Frequency Shift Keying) is the digital version of frequency modulation.
  • Discrete frequency values represent different digital symbols or bits (e.g., $f_1$ = bit 0, $f_2$ = bit 1).
  • Simple to implement and robust to noise — widely used in low-rate wireless (e.g., Bluetooth BLE uses GFSK).

What is the basic idea of Phase Modulation / PSK?
  • Information is encoded in the phase of the carrier signal.
  • In digital form: PSK (Phase Shift Keying) — discrete phase values represent bits/symbols.
  • Examples: BPSK (2 phases → 1 bit/symbol), QPSK (4 phases → 2 bits/symbol).
  • Amplitude remains constant → more robust to amplitude variations than ASK.

"Note"
Why is QAM more bandwidth-efficient than simple AM?
  • QAM uses two orthogonal carriers — the In-phase (I) and Quadrature (Q) channels.
  • Both channels share the same bandwidth, effectively transmitting two independent streams in parallel.
  • This doubles bandwidth efficiency compared to AM, which only uses one carrier dimension.
  • Higher-order QAM (16-QAM, 64-QAM, 256-QAM) further increases bits per symbol by using more constellation points.
  • Lecture 1 – Basics of Wireless Communications
  • Ref: Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) slides (Folie 22)

"Note"
What is the key challenge when using higher-order modulation (e.g., high-order QAM)?
  • Higher-order constellations place symbol points closer together in the signal space.
  • This makes them more sensitive to noise and interference — a small perturbation can cause a symbol error.
  • Requires a better SNR, more linear power amplifiers, and accurate synchronization.
  • Trade-off: more bits/symbol (spectral efficiency) vs. stricter channel quality requirements.
