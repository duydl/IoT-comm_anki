Explain the difference between contention-based and contention-free access in IEEE 802.15.4.
  • Contention-based (CSMA-CA): Devices listen before transmitting; if medium is busy, they wait a random backoff. No timing guarantees.
  • Contention-free (GTS – Guaranteed Time Slots): The coordinator pre-assigns exclusive time slots to specific devices. Provides deterministic, bounded latency — suited for time-sensitive traffic.

In beacon-enabled IEEE 802.15.4, what are the main parts of the superframe, and what is the benefit of the inactive period?
  • Beacon – coordinator frame that starts and defines the superframe.
  • CAP (Contention Access Period) – devices compete using slotted CSMA-CA.
  • CFP (Contention-Free Period) – optional GTS slots assigned by coordinator.
  • Inactive Period – no transmissions; all devices may sleep to save energy.

Why is IEEE 802.15.4 more suitable than Wi-Fi for IoT sensor networks?
  • 802.15.4 is designed for low power consumption — uses duty cycling and sleep periods to extend battery life.
  • Low data rate (max 250 kbit/s) is sufficient for sensor readings.

  • Low complexity and short-range communication suit simple embedded devices.
  • Wi-Fi (802.11) is optimized for high throughput but requires far more energy — unsuitable for coin-cell battery sensors.

What is the modulation / spreading chain for the BPSK PHY in IEEE 802.15.4?
  • Step 1 — Differential encoding: Each bit is encoded relative to the previous bit.
  • Step 2 — Bit-to-chip mapping (DSSS spreading): Each bit is expanded to a chip sequence using a PN code.
  • Step 3 — BPSK modulation: Chip sequence modulates the carrier using binary phase shifts.

"Note"
Why is bit-to-chip mapping used in the 802.15.4 BPSK PHY?
  • Spreads each data bit into a longer chip sequence using a PN code (DSSS).
  • Benefits:
    • Improved interference resistance — narrowband interference affects only a few chips.
    • Makes unauthorized demodulation harder without knowing the spreading code.

What does the lecture say about O-QPSK vs BPSK?
  • O-QPSK (Offset QPSK): Higher bandwidth efficiency — 2 bits/symbol vs 1 bit/symbol for BPSK.
  • Also more resilient: offset between I and Q channels prevents simultaneous transitions → reduced abrupt phase changes → less amplitude variation → more robust to nonlinear amplifiers.
  • O-QPSK is used for the 2.4 GHz PHY in 802.15.4; BPSK for 868 / 915 MHz.
