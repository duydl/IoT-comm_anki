What is the difference between multiplexing and medium access, and why are both needed?
  • Multiplexing: Technique to allow concurrent usage of a medium without interference (SDMA / FDMA / TDMA / CDMA).
  • Medium access: Mechanism that controls how users actually access the medium — often by combining multiplexing methods with scheduling/control rules.
  • Multiplexing provides the resource separation idea; medium access provides the practical control / scheduling on top.

State one key characteristic (or requirement) of each: FDMA, TDMA, and CDMA.
  • FDMA (Frequency Division Multiple Access): Fixed frequency bands are assigned to each channel; no timing coordination needed between users.
  • TDMA (Time Division Multiple Access): Users share the same channel in different time slots; requires synchronization.
  • CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access): Users can share the same frequency and time using orthogonal codes; requires known codes and accurate synchronization plus complex receivers.

In CDMA, why can a receiver recover one sender's bit from the combined sum signal if orthogonal codes are used?
  • The receiver computes the inner product (correlation) of the received signal with the target sender's code.
  • Because all codes are orthogonal, the cross-terms from other users cancel out (sum to zero).
  • The sign of the remaining correlation result reveals the transmitted bit (+1 or −1).

Why is one medium access strategy not enough for all wireless systems?
  • Systems have different requirements: range, traffic load, latency, complexity, synchronization ability, interference level.
  • TDMA: Needs synchronization but gives predictable timing → good for real-time traffic.
  • CDMA: Allows simultaneous users but needs code management and complex receivers.
  • FDMA: Simple conceptually but consumes fixed frequency resources and scales poorly in dense systems.
  • No single strategy optimally trades off latency vs. throughput, energy vs. performance, and simplicity vs. determinism simultaneously.

What is the difference between hard handover and soft handover in cellular networks?
  • Hard handover ("break before make"): The old connection is disconnected before the new one is established. Brief signal loss possible.
  • Soft handover ("make before break"): The new connection is established before disconnecting the old one. Seamless transition; used in CDMA-based systems (e.g., UMTS).

What is the main idea of SDMA, and why is pure space multiplexing usually not enough?
  • In SDMA, a communication channel relates to a specific region or physical infrastructure.
  • Pure space multiplexing is usually not enough because:
    • A secure distance between transmitters is required (to limit interference), and
    • Each channel would need its own transmitting station.
  • → SDMA is mainly useful in combination with other multiplex methods.

What is FDMA?
  • In FDMA (Frequency Division Multiple Access), specific frequencies are permanently assigned to transmission channels.
  • Similar in concept to broadcast radio — each channel occupies a dedicated frequency band at all times.

What determines the bandwidth of a link in FDMA?
  • The assigned frequency band (carrier spacing / allocated band) determines the bandwidth of the link.
  • Narrower bands → lower bandwidth per user; wider bands → higher bandwidth but fewer simultaneous users.

What is TDMA, and what is required for it to work?
  • In TDMA (Time Division Multiple Access), the transmission medium is slot-assigned to channels for certain times.
  • Users share the same frequency, each transmitting only in their assigned time slot.
  • Requires synchronization (timing) between transmitting and receiving stations — can be static or dynamic.

What is the core idea of CDMA?
  • In CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access), orthogonal codes are assigned to channels.
  • Users can transmit on the same frequency at the same time — the codes allow the receiver to separate them.

"Note"
What are the main requirements and challenges of CDMA?
  • Exact synchronization is required between sender and receiver.
  • Receiver must know the sender's code.
  • Complex receivers are needed — they must perform correlation to separate overlapping signals.
  • Noise should not be very high — performance degrades significantly under high noise.

In the CDMA example, how are the transmitted signals from two senders combined on the air?
  • The signals overlay on the air — the combined received signal is the sum of both transmitted code signals: $$C = A_s + B_s$$
  • (Ignoring faults/noise in the ideal example.)
  • Neither sender needs to know the other is transmitting; the receiver handles the separation.

How does a CDMA receiver recover one sender's bit from the combined signal?
  • The receiver uses the target sender's key and computes the inner product (correlation) with the combined signal.
  • If the result is > 0 → sent bit is interpreted as 1.
  • If the result is < 0 → sent bit is interpreted as 0.
  • Orthogonality of other codes ensures their contributions cancel out in the correlation.

What are the major challenges in cellular systems, and what is the main solution?
  • Challenges:
    • Limited frequency resources
    • Interference between cells using the same frequency
  • Solution: Reuse frequency channels in remote (non-adjacent) cells — cells that are far enough apart can share the same frequencies without causing harmful interference.

What is the recommended reuse distance formula in cellular systems?
  • $$D = \sqrt{3N} \cdot R$$
  • Where:
    • $N$ = number of different cell types (cluster size / reuse factor)
    • $R$ = cell radius
    • $D$ = minimum distance between cells using the same frequency
  • Larger $N$ → larger reuse distance → less interference, but fewer simultaneous frequency reuses.

What is the difference between intra-cell and inter-cell handover?
  • Intra-cell handover: Switch-over inside the same cell — e.g., to another frequency or timeslot within the same base station.
  • Inter-cell handover: Switch-over to a neighboring cell — the mobile station connects to a different base station.

What is the point of the frequency assignment examples (large vs. small cells)?
  • They show that different systems and cell sizes use different reuse factors $N$.
  • Changing $N$ changes the reuse ratio $D/R$ — affecting:
    • Interference level between co-channel cells
    • Capacity planning (how many independent frequency sets are needed)
  • Smaller cells can reuse frequencies more aggressively (smaller $N$), increasing capacity but requiring more careful interference management.
